Research Methods and Professional Practice

This page outlines my progress throughout the Research Methods and Professional Practice (RMPP) module. Narratives of each unit follow below, including my weekly reflections, activities performed, and links to artefacts.

Unit 1: Scientific Investigation and Ethics

Reflection: This module began by introducing the concept of academic research and, most importantly, underscoring the importance of ethics in the field. Many reading assignments in this unit identified the distinction between ethics and morality, which has helped me understand my own position. Though ethics are critical to the respect and discipline in our field, I feel that morality has a special place in the decision-making process and ultimately determines how well I’ll sleep at night. As I read about scientific investigation and research, the equities of ethics and morality were generally aligned. However, when reading about conducting offensive cyber operations to disable predatory networks, ethics and morality certainly clashed.

    Activities Performed:
  • Selected a research topic for the Literature Review and Presentation assignments.
  • Conducted research and developed an Initial Post for Collaborative Discussion 1 (link to Collaborative Discussion 1).
  • Completed the Reasoning Quiz.
  • Completed Reflective Activity 1 – Ethics in Computing (link to Reflective Activity 1).

Unit 2: Research Questions, the Literature Review and the Research Proposal

Reflection: This week introduced literature reviews and research proposals. Though I have read hundreds of literature reviews over the years, I have never produced one – so I enjoyed taking a moment to understand the underlying methodology and degree of labour required. Similarly, I have written scores of business proposals over the years, but never a research proposal. As with other types of proposals, I learned that research proposals must clearly describe your goal, the means by which you will achieve that goal, and why your approach will work. This has reinforced my appreciation for creative approaches to problem solving – research, or otherwise – and the use of effective communication to obtain audience buy-in.

    Activities Performed:
  • Listened to the “Research Questions, the Literature Review and the Research Proposal” Lecturecast.
  • Read and responded to classmates’ posts for Collaborative Discussion 1 (link to Collaborative Discussion 1).

Unit 3: Research Methods

Reflection: This was one of my favourite units throughout my MSc programme. Research design allows me to figure out how to solve a research problem, using different modalities to learn about a problem. This week’s reading assignments reinforced my notion that research methods offer different ways of collecting and synthesising information from data, with each method bringing its own strengths and weaknesses. I learned that combining research methodologies produces a multi-dimensional perspective of a problem, and ultimately delivers more meaningful and accurate results. I believe that research design is an art as much as it is a science, and benefits from a holistic understanding of the problem.

Unit 4: Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

Reflection: : During Unit 4, I learned about different observation methods to conduct primary research. For my literature review, I have decided to research the application of machine learning in music recommender systems (MRS); specifically, I want to determine if the use of MRS impacts the musical preferences of its users. I believe that many of the observation methods discussed in this week – such as focus groups and case studies – may be prone to bias through subjective interpretations and anecdotal evidence. The more I learn about music preference formation, the more I see a need to isolate subjective variables and create apples-to-apples quantitative comparisons. Furthermore, it seems that many studies in this field may inadvertently impact the results – so discreet observation will be important.

    Activities Performed:

Unit 5: User Experience

Reflection: User Experience (UX) is a field that has always fascinated me, especially as a user. This unit showed me how far the field has come in the past few years, with more attention and resources dedicated to UX efforts throughout the entire project lifecycle. Though UX ultimately relies upon the fundamental concept of delivering user requirements, it is enlightening to see how user behaviour is increasingly considered from requirements gathering through post-implementation evaluation. I had previously associated “UX” with “UI,” under the false impression that UX was predominantly aesthetic. However, I believe that effective UX has more in common with behavioural science than software development. Further, UX can help advocate for user equities during the development phase.

Unit 6: pytest and Test-Driven Development

Reflection: Unit 6 introduced Test-Driven Development (TDD), a development methodology I had never personally encountered. Admittedly, automated testing has been a weakness of mine; throughout my development projects, I have always produced custom tests making extensive use of the print function to see what was happening in the program and to debug errors. This week gave me more exposure to additional automated testing concepts, additional exposure to pytest, and showed me how testing can be used to advance development instead of occurring as a perfunctory afterthought.

Unit 7: Software Development Life Cycles

Reflection: This week revisited many common concepts discussed earlier in this module, and throughout the MSc educational programme, in the context of the software development life cycle (SDLC). This unit tied many concepts together and helped me enumerate and evaluate the many SDLCs in practice today. Though Agile is the preferred approach for most contemporary projects, it’s useful to see how predictive SDLCs can help structure and guide development efforts. I am particularly interested in combining these SDLCs as appropriate, having witnessed successful hybrid Agile/waterfall and Agile/Scrum/Kanban SDLCs in recent years.

Unit 9: Quality Management Strategy

Reflection: Unit 9 introduced the concept of quality as a process throughout all phases of software engineering project management. Whilst I was familiar with the incorporation of quality assurance and quality control, using quality planning as a means of preventing technical debt was a novel concept. As I learned with testing, incorporating quality management throughout all phases of the SDLC should be a deliberate process that ultimately benefits project cost, schedule, and customer satisfaction. This week’s reading also gave me quality management metrics that I can apply to my current projects.

Unit 10: Software Quality Monitoring in Python

Reflection: My knowledge of Python has steadily increased from module to module. Unit 10 revisited linters as a best practice to analyse code quality, which I have used extensively throughout my studies. This week, I learned how to incorporate linters through GitHub actions as part of a continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) pipeline, courtesy of teammate Marianne. It was impressive to see how CI/CD pipelines can quickly and efficiently ascertain the quality and security of code, and something I intend to use in my future development projects.

Reflection: This was a busy week for the module, as we put the final touches on our Presentation assignment for submission. It was interesting to learn about future trends in software engineering project management, particularly in the realm of Artificial Intelligence Operations, or AIOps, which may emerge as the next evolution of Development Operations (DevOps). I also learned about the next generation of languages emerging, and the additional security measures they incorporate; I have already begun to investigate the Rust language.

Unit 12: The Case for the Future Direction of Software Engineering Project Management

Reflection: This week built upon the emerging software engineering trends introduced in Unit 11. Our group discussed the likelihood of each trend to significantly impact the field of study; however, each of these was overshadowed by the recent proliferation of ChatGPT and the emerging field of language training model AI platforms, and how they may be used to generate and assess the quality of code. Though the hype around these technologies is deafening, along with a general failure to understand how the technology works, the case for using ChatGPT to assist software development is undeniable. Just ask it.

Useful Resources

During this module, my team and I used the following tools the most:
  • Adobe Premiere – one of the industry standard video editing applications. This has a free trial option, but better yet, Adobe offers the entire Creative Cloud Suite at a drastically discounted rate for students https://www.adobe-students.com/creativecloud/buy/students.html
  • GitHub – the gold standard for version control systems and developing across a team. During this unit, we also used the GitHub Project and Actions to manage tasks and automate CI/CD: https://github.com/
  • Lucidchart – offers a free tier for students that is full featured but limited to 3 simultaneous documents. My favourite tool for UML, by far: https://www.lucidchart.com/pages/usecase/education
  • JetBrains PyCharm – a great IDE for Python that supports many plug-ins and extensions, including Git integration. Jetbrains offers students free use of their software: https://www.jetbrains.com/community/education/#students
  • Notepad++ - a great, simple, FREE text editor for Windows. Supports multiple tabs, sophisticated editing of any file that’s text based: https://notepad-plus-plus.org/"
  • Slack – a collaborative platform key to enabling development across different locations and timezones. This is a highly flexible, feature-rich communications platform that offers its key functionality for free, and also has a premium tier useful for businesses or larger projects : https://slack.com/
  • Sublime Text – my favourite text editor on Mac. Like Notepad ++ but with a sleeker interface, and slightly fewer features. Trial version available for free: https://www.sublimetext.com
