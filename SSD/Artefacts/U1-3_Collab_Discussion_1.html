Secure Software Development, Unit 1-3

Collaborative Discussion 1: UML Flowchart

Summary Post

In 2017, OWASP rated “Using Components with Known Vulnerabilities” as its ninth most significant weakness; in the 2021, this weakness was elevated to the sixth position and renamed “Vulnerable and Outdated Components” to reflect a broader threat within this category (MITRE, 2018; OWASP, 2021b). Furthermore, OWASP’s Top 10 Community Survey cited this issue as the second worst weakness in the field, fueled by the sustained growth of open source and third-party components in software development (Armerding, 2019; OWASP, 2021a). A prime example of hackers exploiting such a vulnerability occurred in 2017, when credit reporting firm Equifax suffered a breach impacting 143 million customers’ data (Brewster, 2017). Equifax’s implementation of Apache Struts had not been updated to fix a vulnerability with a Java parser, despite the fact that a patch had been released several months before (Armerding, 2019; Brewster, 2017).

Unified Modeling Language (UML) Sequence Diagrams show interactions between objects, their sequential order, and activation of those objects (Bell, 2004). This construct depicts the causational relationships between actions: as Developers build complex systems without maintaining a software component inventory, those components may become obsolete or vulnerable to attack (Armerding, 2019). Attackers then conduct reconnaissance operations to identify and exploit these vulnerabilities (Armerding, 2019; OWASP, 2021b). As developers continue to develop and enhance systems without properly inventorying and updating software components, the number of vulnerabilities continues to grow – facilitating future reconnaissance and attack cycles.

Dr. Peoples commented that this description and diagram effectively conveyed this pattern (Peoples, 2022). Marianne discussed the merits of having an organizational approval process for the use of open-source software, and how companies should maintain tools and systems to track use of open source libraries to mitigate vulnerabilities such as Log4j (Manaog, 2022).
